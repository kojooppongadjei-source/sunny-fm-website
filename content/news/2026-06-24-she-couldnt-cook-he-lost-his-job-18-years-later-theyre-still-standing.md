It started with a social media post.

Evangeline Gbenartey wasn't looking for attention when she wrote it. She simply sat down and listed — honestly, without polish — 18 challenges she and her husband Isaac had walked through together. Unemployment. Expectations that turned out to be illusions. A wife who, despite coming from a family of brilliant cooks, had no idea how to boil rice when she got married.

The post took off. Nearly 50,000 views. Hundreds of comments from people who recognised something in it — relief, perhaps, that someone had the courage to say what marriage actually looks like from the inside.

It was that post that brought the couple to the sofa on Sunny FM's *IM Drive with Jennifer J Danquah*, where they sat down to tell the fuller story.

- - -

## "God Used That Season to Bind Us Together"

The first years of marriage are supposed to be the golden years. For Isaac and Evangeline, they were also the years a job disappeared.

Shortly after they wed, Evangeline lost her employment. It came without warning and landed hard. The financial pressure was real, but what Isaac remembers most is the confusion — the quiet, unsettling question of *why*. Why them. Why now.

Time gave him an answer he wasn't expecting.

*"What felt like discomfort then became the foundation for greater intimacy and understanding,"* he said.

Stripped of the busyness that employment brings, they were left with each other. Long conversations. Shared uncertainty. A friendship, forged not in the easy seasons but in the difficult ones, that became the bedrock of everything that followed.

- - -

## The Cooking Confession

Of all the things Evangeline shared that resonated with people, perhaps none raised more eyebrows — or more laughter — than the cooking.

She grew up in a household where food was an art form. Aunties and mothers who could feed a crowd without a recipe. And yet, she entered her marriage not knowing how to cook.

Isaac, to his credit, didn't lead with that when asked what drew him to Evangeline. He saw her character. Her values. The kind of woman she was.

But the children were less diplomatic.

When her kids started giving her honest, unhesitating feedback about her meals — the kind of feedback only children can deliver — something shifted in Evangeline. She decided to learn. Properly. She prayed about it. She called her mother. She listened to the Holy Spirit and to whoever else could help.

Today, Isaac calls home from work to make sure there is food left *for him* before the children finish it.

*"She's an amazing cook now,"* he says, with the satisfaction of a man who has clearly benefited from the transformation.

- - -

## The Expectation Nobody Warns You About

Evangeline walked into marriage with a picture in her mind. A good picture, drawn from what she had observed growing up. Her parents' marriage. The rhythms, the roles, the way love looked in her childhood home.

It took her years to realise she wasn't recreating that picture. She was building something new.

*"I didn't marry my father, and I wasn't becoming my mother,"* she said simply.

Every marriage is its own thing. Its own language. Its own pace. The moment she stopped holding her husband up against the image of her father — an unfair comparison no man could ever win — was the moment she began to truly see who Isaac actually was.

Isaac had his own version of the same lesson.

He had believed that choosing the right person was the hard part. That once you had found each other, love would flow naturally. Romance would be easy. Communication would just happen.

It didn't.

*"Romance is not automatic. Communication is not automatic. Intimacy is not automatic. Everything requires intentional effort,"* he said.

That word — *intentional* — would come up again and again throughout the conversation.

- - -

## One Car, One Decision, Everything Changed

At some point, Isaac and Evangeline made a practical decision that turned out to be one of the most important investments in their marriage.

They chose to commute to work together. One car. Same journey, every day.

What sounds like a simple logistical arrangement became something else entirely — a daily protected space where they prayed together, read together, talked about their dreams, processed their stress, and simply existed in each other's company without agenda.

They added weekly dates to the rhythm. Not extravagant ones. Just consistent ones.

*"These habits were not always easy,"* Isaac acknowledged. There were seasons where the drive felt heavy. Where the conversation was difficult. But they showed up for each other anyway, and it changed things.

- - -

## What a Phone Reminder Said Without Words

During the interview, Isaac told a story quietly — the kind of story you sit with for a while after you've heard it.

He had picked up Evangeline's phone one day. On it, he found a recurring reminder. Not a shopping list. Not a meeting alert.

Just six words: *Pray for Isaac Gbenartey.*

Set to repeat. Every day.

He didn't say much about how it made him feel. He didn't need to.

Evangeline had been talking about the weight men carry — the pressure to provide, to hold things together, to keep going even when they're running on empty. She spoke about the power of simple affirmation. Of saying *I see you. I'm proud of you. I'm praying for you.* Of making a man feel like someone at home is in his corner.

That phone reminder was all of that, said in silence, six words at a time.

- - -

## When to Ask for Help

The Gbenarteys are honest about the fact that they didn't figure all of this out alone.

They spoke candidly about post-marital counselling — something they believe more couples should pursue. Not just the sessions before the wedding, but the guidance that comes after, when the real questions start to surface.

Evangeline offered a word of wisdom here: be careful who you let into your marriage. Not every well-meaning person will give objective counsel. Not every confidant will keep a confidence. Choose wisely. Seek professional, unbiased guidance from people who can see your situation clearly without agenda.

- - -

## What 18 Years Actually Teaches You

As the conversation drew to a close, Isaac said something that has stayed with us.

*"Marriage is always changing. When the season changes, seek the wisdom you need to navigate it."*

Not a formula. Not a five-step plan. Just an honest acknowledgement that the marriage you have at year two is not the marriage you'll have at year ten, and the one at year ten is not what you'll be navigating at year eighteen.

Each season asks something different of you. And each season, if you're paying attention, gives you something too.

- - -

After eighteen years, Isaac and Evangeline Gbenartey are still learning. Still investing. Still choosing each other — deliberately, imperfectly, faithfully.

Their story doesn't promise that marriage is easy. It promises something better: that the hard parts don't have to be the end of the story.

*Keep learning. Keep loving. Keep praying. And never stop investing in each other.*
